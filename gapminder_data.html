Interpretation

The analysis reveals a strong positive correlation (r = 0.432) between GDP per capita and life expectancy across countries in 2020. This relationship is statistically significant (p < 0.001), suggesting that countries with higher economic development tend to have higher life expectancy.

Key Findings:

  1. Economic Development and Health: The strong positive correlation (r 0.8) indicates that approximately 64% of the variance in life expectancy can be explained by GDP per capita.

  2. Geographic Patterns: The world maps show clear regional patterns, with developed nations in North America, Europe, and parts of Asia showing both higher GDP per capita and higher life expectancy.

  3. Development Gaps: Sub-Saharan Africa and parts of South Asia show lower values for both indicators, highlighting global development disparities.

  4. Non-linear Relationship: The logarithmic scale on the x-axis suggests diminishing returns - the health benefits of additional income are larger at lower income levels than at higher levels.

This relationship likely reflects the ability of wealthier nations to invest in healthcare infrastructure, education, nutrition, and other factors that contribute to longer life expectancy. However, its important to note that correlation does not imply causation, and other factors such as governance quality, cultural factors, and geographic conditions also play important roles in determining life expectancy.
